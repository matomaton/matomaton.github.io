Scheduler · AI Experience

Chatbot Interaction Design Guidelines

Principles for developers, data scientists, and interaction designers. Industry-agnostic: travel, logistics, trucking, hospitality, manufacturing visits, and beyond.

Status
🟡 Draft — for review and comment
Author
Matthew Martin, Product Design
Scope
8 guidelines · 5-phase model

Purpose and scope

This document defines the interaction design principles and guidelines that govern how the Scheduler application's AI chatbot should behave. It is written for the development team, data scientists, and AI model engineers who are building the experience.

The guidelines are deliberately principle-level rather than feature-level. Individual features will differ across verticals — a trucking dispatcher's canvas looks different from a leisure traveller's itinerary — but the underlying rules around transparency, user authority, coercion, and tone apply everywhere.

Core design hypothesis. Users struggle to articulate preferences in the abstract. When shown a concrete first-draft plan, they immediately know what is wrong. The AI's role is not to ask what the user wants — it is to show something good, then help the user make it theirs.

The five-phase conversation model

Every scheduler session follows this sequence. Phases 3 and 4 form an iterative loop that repeats until the user is satisfied.

# Phase What happens Who leads
1 Orient Collect the three Minimum Viable Context fields: When, Where, Who. Nothing more. AI asks · User provides
2 Generate AI produces a complete first-draft dossier: canvas, calendar, and inline rationale for every item. AI leads
3 React User evaluates the plan. Three scaffolded prompts lower the entry barrier: Affirm / Change / Remove. User leads
4 Refine Iterative edits: swap, reschedule, delete, lock. Loop back to React as many times as needed. User leads · AI executes
5 Confirm Finalized dossier. User approves. Any consequential actions (bookings, purchases) require explicit sign-off. User confirms

Important: The AI must never skip to Phase 2 without collecting all three MVC fields, and must never linger in Phase 1 longer than necessary. As soon as When, Where, and Who are complete — generate the draft.

G1
Show first, converse second
Phases: Orient → Generate  ·  Minimum viable context

Core principle

The AI should never open a conversation with open-ended questions. Collect only the minimum information needed to generate a plausible first draft, then produce it immediately. The plan is the conversation starter — not a questionnaire.

The Minimum Viable Context (MVC) rule

Three fields unlock the first draft. Nothing else is required at this stage.

WhenDates, times, and duration of the schedule.
WhereOrigin, destination, or operating area.
WhoNumber of people, roles, or special requirements.
All other preferences are captured through the reaction loop in Phases 3 and 4 — not upfront.

Do / Avoid

✓ Do this

"I've built a 3-day Chicago itinerary for two people. Here's my best guess — what did I get right, and what would you change?"

✗ Avoid this

"What kind of activities do you like? What's your budget? Do you prefer morning or evening starts? Any dietary preferences?"

Implementation note: Enforce the MVC rule at the API level. Reject generation calls missing any of the three fields with a structured validation error — do not silently fill in defaults.

G2
Transparent reasoning, woven inline
Phase: Generate  ·  Trust infrastructure

Core principle

Every AI-suggested item must carry a plain-language reason. This is not a debug log — it is trust infrastructure. Users who understand why the AI chose something can evaluate it, accept it, or override it with confidence.

Rationale format

"Suggested because [user signal or context fact]."

One sentence. Maximum 120 characters. Displayed as secondary text beneath the item title in both canvas and calendar views — never hidden behind a tooltip, "i" icon, or separate drawer.

Do / Avoid

✓ Do this

"Art Institute of Chicago — suggested because you indicated an interest in history and it is 0.3 miles from your hotel."

✗ Avoid this

Show a suggested item with no explanation, or put the reasoning in a separate "AI thinking" panel that users never open.

Commercial bias rule: If a suggestion is influenced by a partner, referral fee, or promotion, it must be disclosed with a visible "Sponsored" label. Undisclosed commercial weighting violates the user's trust and the fiduciary duty of the AI.

G3
Mixed-initiative handover at decision boundaries
Phases: Refine → Confirm  ·  Automation tiers

Core principle

Define two tiers of AI authority. The AI may act autonomously on Routine actions. It must pause and ask on Consequential ones. This boundary is non-negotiable and must be enforced in code, not just convention.
TierDefinitionExamples
Routine AI may act without asking Reordering stops · adjusting timing · suggesting alternatives · updating rationale text
Consequential User must explicitly approve Financial transactions · bookings · significant plan deviations · irreversible actions

The pause-and-offer pattern

When an external signal (weather, availability change, traffic) forces a replanning decision, the AI must present 2–4 options with trade-offs and wait for the user to choose. It must never silently pick one.

✓ Do this

"Rain is forecast on Day 2. I can swap the outdoor market for: (A) the Art Institute, (B) a cooking class, or (C) keep the original and revisit. What would you prefer?"

✗ Avoid this

Silently replace the outdoor market with the nearest indoor attraction and present a "done" state — even if the replacement is objectively better.

Logistics / trucking note: Consequential actions include route changes that add cost, stops that alter delivery windows, or load reassignments. The AI proposes; the dispatcher confirms. Every approval event must be logged with a timestamp and the options that were presented.

G4
No dark patterns, no coercive language
All phases  ·  Ethical guardrail

Core principle

The chatbot must never use urgency, scarcity, guilt, or social pressure to override a user's intent. These patterns exploit cognitive load and manufacture anxiety. In a scheduling context — which is about purposeful planning — they are inappropriate regardless of vertical.

Banned language patterns — QA must verify these are absent

URGENCY "Only 2 tickets left — book now!" when no genuine scarcity data exists
GUILT "I'd hate for you to miss this — are you sure?" used as a persuasion tactic
FOMO "Everyone visiting Chicago goes here" used as social pressure
NAGGING Re-suggesting a dismissed item more than once without new information
EXIT BLOCK "Are you really sure you want to stop?" when a user dismisses a suggestion

Do / Avoid

✓ Do this

"The Art Institute tends to sell out on weekends — you may want to reserve in advance if you're going Saturday." (Factual, useful, not pressured.)

✗ Avoid this

"Only 3 spots left — I'd feel terrible if you missed this! Book now before it's gone!"

Dismissal rule: When a user dismisses or ignores a suggestion, record it. Do not resurface the item unless a new informational signal is present (e.g. price drop, availability change). The resurface must carry that new signal as its rationale. One follow-up with new information is acceptable. Repeated re-prompting is not.

G5
User-configurable guardrails
Phases: Orient → Generate  ·  Standing policies

Core principle

Users should be able to set standing rules that permanently shape the AI's behaviour without repeating them every session. These are not filters — they are policies. The AI honours them proactively; it does not wait for the user to catch a violation.

Example policy types

BUDGET "Never suggest paid activities over $20 per person"
CONTENT "Skip museums — I always prefer outdoor activities"
PACING "No more than two structured activities per day"
COMMERCIAL "Do not surface sponsored or promoted suggestions"
TIMING "Never schedule anything before 9am"

Learning from the reaction loop

The system should detect stable deletion patterns across sessions — for example, if the user consistently removes all nightlife items across three or more trips — and propose a policy: "It looks like you prefer daytime-only activities. Should I make that a standing rule?"

Critical rule: The system must never activate an inferred policy automatically. The user must explicitly confirm before it becomes active. Inferred and user-set policies must be visually distinguished in the preferences panel.

G6
Reaction scaffolding and structured prompts
Phase: React  ·  Conversation design

Core principle

When presenting the first draft, the AI must not end with an open "What do you think?" — this returns users to the blank-slate problem. Instead, provide structured reaction prompts that lower the cognitive cost of engaging with the plan.

The reaction scaffold (standard template)

After presenting the draft, always surface these three entry points:

✓ Affirm"What did I get right — should I lock any of this in?"
↔ Change"What would you swap, move, or change?"
✕ Remove"Is there anything you'd cut entirely?"

Implicit intent signals — for the NLP / ML team

The model must recognise colloquial reaction signals and map them to structured edit operations:

User saysSystem operation
"I'd never do a museum on a vacation"DELETE_ITEM + CHANGE_PREFERENCE → propose standing policy
"Make Day 2 a bit lighter"REDUCE_DENSITY on Day 2 items
"Can we move dinner earlier?"RESCHEDULE_ITEM (dinner slot)
"What else could I do near the hotel?"REQUEST_ALTERNATIVES (proximity filter)
"Keep the morning hike — love that"LOCK_ITEM (morning hike)

NLP target: Intent classifier failure rate should be below 5% on a test bank of 50 representative user messages before production release. Log all unmapped intents as training candidates.

G7
Contextual integrity across verticals
All phases  ·  Tone and norms configuration

Core principle

The AI's tone and urgency level must match the social context of the activity being scheduled. A leisure trip has different norms than a logistics run. The same chatbot behaviour that feels helpful in one context can feel invasive or inappropriate in another.

Vertical tone matrix

VerticalToneUrgency normNotes
Travel / tourism Warm, exploratory, unhurried None unless factually grounded Leisure is the purpose. No pressure tactics.
Logistics / trucking Efficient, precise, operational Appropriate when genuine — time and cost deltas are primary Compliance mode available for regulated routes.
Hotel / hospitality Attentive, personal, anticipatory None unless factually grounded Upsells only if policy-permitted; never as pressure.
Manufacturing visits Structured, formal, time-bounded Operational — compliance-aware No soft or persuasive language. All deviations must be confirmed.

Configuration rule: Vertical context is set at the application configuration level by the operator — it is not inferred per conversation. A missing or malformed VerticalConfig must fail loudly on application startup, not silently default to a generic tone. Wrong vertical tone is an ethical issue, not just a UX bug.

G8
Measurable interaction quality signals
All phases  ·  Product analytics and data science

Core principle

Avoid optimising for engagement proxies (session length, click-throughs) that can be inflated through dark patterns. Measure signals that reflect genuine user value.

The five quality metrics

MetricDefinitionWhat it tells us
Plan adoption rate % of AI-generated items kept unmodified to the Confirm stage First-draft quality and relevance
Reaction depth Average number of specific edits made per session Richness of engagement with the plan
Guardrail satisfaction % of users rating their active policies as accurate How well the inference pipeline learns preferences
First-draft accuracy % of draft assumptions left uncorrected by the user How well MVC signals translate to good defaults
Coercion signal rate Resurfaced dismissed items ÷ total sessions Early warning for dark pattern drift — alert at >2%

Anti-metric: Do not use session length, message count, or click-through rate as success metrics. These can all be inflated by dark patterns. A user who refines their plan in 4 exchanges and leaves satisfied is a better outcome than a user who spends 20 minutes in open-ended dialogue and abandons. Document the excluded metrics explicitly so they are not quietly re-added during roadmap reviews.

Notes for reviewers

This document is a working draft. Your feedback is invited on all sections. The following questions are intended to guide targeted review — please add comments directly in Confluence against the relevant section.

  1. G1 — MVC scope. Is the Minimum Viable Context rule (When / Where / Who) the right scope for first-draft generation in your vertical? Are there cases where fewer or more inputs are genuinely needed before a useful draft is possible?
  2. G3 — Automation tier classification. Is the Routine / Consequential boundary correctly defined? Are there action types in your vertical that should be reclassified in either direction?
  3. G4 — Banned pattern list. Are there urgency or scarcity communication patterns that are legitimately appropriate for your vertical that this list would incorrectly prevent?
  4. G6 — Intent-to-operation mapping. Do the five example mappings in the NLP table cover the most common user messages in your vertical? What colloquial phrases are missing from the coverage?
  5. G7 — Vertical tone matrix. Is your vertical's tone and urgency profile correctly described? Are there additional verticals that should be added before the first release?
  6. G8 — Quality metrics. Are the five quality metrics the right ones to baseline before launch? Are there additional signals specific to your vertical or team that should be tracked from day one?
